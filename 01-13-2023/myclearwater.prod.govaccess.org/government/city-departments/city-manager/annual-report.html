The city of Clearwater is proud to present its Fiscal Year 2020/21 Annual Report, which covers Oct. 1, 2020, through Sept. 30, 2021.

This Year's Successes are Dedicated to City Manager Bill Horne

As many of you already know, our city and the Clearwater community has lost an amazing man, our beloved City Manager Bill Horne. He died Saturday, Aug. 14, of a suspected heart attack. The Clearwater community lost a great city manager and an amazing man that day, and many of us also lost a good friend and mentor as well.

After serving dozens of mayors and council members during his tenure, Bill Horne led our city as city manager for more than two decades.

“What I’m most proud of is I’ve left the city better than I found it,” said Horne, back in April 2021, when asked about his biggest accomplishment.

A retired U.S. Air Force Colonel, he began his career with the city of Clearwater in 1998 as general support services administrator. The next year, he became assistant city manager. In July 2000, he was named interim city manager and then became city manager the following year.

Throughout his term, Horne led the city with skill and drive. Through it all, he remained a steadfast, strong leader on whom residents and staff could rely. The city will miss his steady hand and unwavering leadership.

This year's successes and accomplishments are dedicated to the memory of Mr. Horne and his legacy. After all, a lot of items listed on this webpage had Mr. Horne’s fingerprints all over them.

While many still grieve his passing, city leaders and staff have continued to move the city forward, never forgetting the values that mattered most to him: Responsibility. Integrity. Empathy.

Imagine Clearwater Launches

It was a banner year for the highly-anticipated Imagine Clearwater project on the waterfront in Downtown Clearwater with the launch of construction on the $84-million redevelopment of the city’s iconic Coachman Park.

Imagine Clearwater is an investment in the redevelopment of Downtown Clearwater and its waterfront – including construction of a new 4,000-seat covered amphitheater in Coachman Park – that will be a must-see destination point in the Tampa Bay area.

The magnitude of the project is evidenced by the rolling in of roughly 65,000 cubic yards of dirt to the construction site in recent weeks on more than 4,000 trucks. The material is being used to elevate the site to lift new buildings out of the flood zone and to accommodate new grading needed for design features.

Excitement over the project continues to build as the public can watch construction online giving the region a birds-eye view of progress on this game-changing project. To view 24-hour live webcams overlooking the Imagine construction site visit imagineclearwater.com.

“Imagine Clearwater is a generational project, one that we believe will serve Clearwater families and guests for decades to come. Finally, we will be taking full advantage of our unique downtown waterfront,” said Clearwater Mayor Frank Hibbard.

Designed to connect the waterfront and the Downtown Clearwater community, Imagine Clearwater includes an expansive park and recreational spaces, a gateway plaza and bluff walk that connects the park to downtown, a bay walk promenade overlooking the Intracoastal Waterway, a lake area with picnic shelters, and an ocean-themed play area with an interactive pop-jet water feature.

The additions to Coachman Park also include the amphitheater which will accommodate a year-round performance schedule that promises to continue the city of Clearwater’s reputation as a premier location for diverse live entertainment.

For more information on the project, to view renderings on the project, and view construction live, visit imagineclearwater.com.

Our Next Steps for 2022

This year, our organization will welcome and adjust to new leadership. The city coordinated the hiring of our next city manager, Jon Jennings from Portland, Maine (shown), and he will start with us Nov. 8.

Clearwater's new city attorney, David Margolis, came to the city from Orlando and started employment in late October.

City goals for the coming year include the strategic planning process, public safety, and bringing to life the Imagine Clearwater project.

Ongoing efforts to revitalize Downtown Clearwater will continue, and the city would like to solidify future plans for our next City Hall location.

And of course, the city will continue to focus on providing high-quality core municipal services that our residents have come to expect each and every day.

 

The 2020/21 budget was $637,412,017 for citywide services, an increase of $81.9 million across all city operations. The General Fund budget reflected expenditures of $154,415,831, a decrease of less than 1 percent from the 2019/20 budget of $154,650,253. The city’s approved millage rate is 5.955 mills. 

Our Financial Health

The Clearwater City Council has required minimum fund balances for all of its funds. While the final financial report will not be out until later this year, the city is pleased report that all of our funds are currently in compliance with that requirement.

This is due in great part to the strategic approach to how the city has planned and implemented its operating and capital budgets. As in past years, staff was very focused on reviewing departmental operations to ensure they met the requirements of the strategic direction and carefully balancing the need to meet the community’s expectations with the available resource needs and the need for long-term financial stability.

Top COVID-19 Accomplishments

Express Library Services

The Clearwater Public Library System pivoted from their traditional services to provide express library services. It provided curbside service and expanded its drive-through service so that the public could access library resources. The library also developed a rich offering of virtual programs and free take-and-make crafts, to allow patrons to use library resources while safe distancing. These actions embody our strategic directions of “Safety” and “Foster Community Engagement.”

Financial Support To Struggling Residents

The city provided financial support to some Clearwater residents who are struggling financially, as a result of the pandemic. Throughout last fiscal year, the Economic Development & Housing Department administered an Emergency Rent, Mortgage & Utility Assistance program, with assistance from staff from the library, Utility Customers Service, and Finance departments. The program assisted 219 Clearwater households using CARES Act funds through the Florida Housing Finance Corporation. The city provided more than $127,000 in mortgage assistance and $983,500 in rental assistance. We paid more than $13,900 in security deposits, $874 in storage fees and almost $186,700 in utility payments.

In addition, the Utility Customer Service Department teamed up with the Salvation Army to help customers in need to pay their utility bills. This assistance totaled more than $16,000 through our We Care Fund.

Together, these programs afforded us the opportunity to help some of our residents who are struggling financially due to the pandemic. This work showcases our strategic direction of “Fiscal Responsibility” and “Foster Community Engagement.”

Strong Partnerships

Central to the city's COVID-19 efforts were our partnerships. COVID does not care about jurisdictional borders, and this required us and our partners to put aside our differences and be in lock step. Emergency manager Jevon Graham and his response team spent a lot of time this year cultivating partnerships with Pinellas County, our neighboring municipalities, the Department of Health, and countless others. These partnerships put Clearwater in a position to host COVID-19 testing sites and multiple vaccination sites, as well as a monoclonal antibody infusion site. Additionally, the city provided significant support in the operation of these sites through its police and fire and rescue staff.

These important partnerships provided critical service to residents in northern Pinellas County, while also ensuring a high level of accessibility to those services to residents of Clearwater. This accomplishment embodies our strategic direction of “Safety.”

Health Fair Week

The Human Resources Department shifted from its typical city employees’ health fair and reimagined it as Health Fair Week. At Health Fair Week, employees and retirees had the opportunity to register for various preventive health screenings, such as for bone density, biometrics, and skin cancer checks. Employees also could sign up for a drive-by flu shot or COVID vaccine. Health Fair Week not only kicked off open enrollment, but it also gave employees access to several health and wellness resources. This accomplishment ties into our strategic direction of “Safety,” “Quality,” and “Financial Responsibility.”

Employee Safety & Support

The city supported its employees during the pandemic and worked hard to keep them safe. Staff from many city departments, including the Human Resources Department, continued to remain agile and responsive to support staff issues and needs, relating to the COVID-19 crisis. Managers and other city leaders handled personnel sickness notifications, quarantine, self-isolation and COVID-19 testing for our employees.

Throughout the pandemic, our leaders have maintained a consistent approach and took proactive steps to adhere to CDC guidelines. Therefore, the staff positivity rate for COVID-19 has remained relatively low throughout our organization. Out of approximately 1,900 city positions, the positivity rate among employees has stayed at only 383, since the beginning of the pandemic. To date, the city has prevented mass outbreaks among staff and departments.

Together, these actions represent our strategic direction of “Safety.”

Other COVID-19 Accomplishments
  • Implemented COVID-19 protocols, such as daily mask wearing, safe distancing, and obtaining proper personal protective equipment for sickness-related calls. The city handled personnel sickness notifications, quarantine, self-isolation and COVID-19 testing for all city departments.
  • Maintained a face covering policy and symptom-based, stay-at-home policy, which also helped keep the city positivity rate to a minimum.
  • Continues to supply masks, sanitizers and personal protective equipment to all city employees.
  • Maintained protective barriers throughout the city and in high-traffic areas, where employees come in contact with the public.
  • Purchased and provided cleaning supplies for all city departments when supplies were limited.
  • Provided deep cleaning to city facilities to help sanitize surfaces in high-traffic areas.
  • Aided countywide vaccination efforts by providing city paramedics to help administer COVID vaccines at city sites.
  • Due to our consistent approach and proactive implementation of CDC-based policy, only 383 out of our approximately 1,900 employees have tested positive since the beginning of the pandemic.
  • Provided employees who wish to get vaccinated the opportunity to get the vaccine at the Employee Health Center.
  • Strategically placed portable restrooms in several places to assist people experiencing street-level homelessness when business and public buildings were mandated to close. The city also provided laundry and shower facilities at a strategic location for use by homeless individuals.
  • An increase in COVID-related cases resulted in fire departments transporting patients to hospitals countywide; transports typically are performed by Sunstar, an ambulance service. Clearwater Fire & Rescue instituted and acquired the necessary patient transportation guidelines and equipment to deliver patients in a timely manner to hospitals while following county protocols.
  • Remained agile and responsive regarding support of staff issues and needs, as they related to the COVID-19 crisis.
  • Maintained uninterrupted commercial solid waste collection service, seven days a week, since the start of the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Provided uninterrupted maintenance and emergency response 24/7 throughout the pandemic on HVAC, plumbing, emergency power and critical information systems, including maintenance and preventative maintenance on more than 70 generators.

City's Top Accomplishments in Regular Business

Greenprint 2.0

This year, the city adopted Clearwater Greenprint 2.0, with the goal of advancing our sustainability goals. The new plan aims to reduce citywide greenhouse gas emissions to 80 percent below baseline levels citywide by 2050. The Greenprint program promotes our strategic directions of “Efficiency,” “Quality,” “Foster Community Engagement,” “Financial Responsibility,” and “Diversify the Economic Base.”

Fleet Improvements

Clearwater made several improvements to its fleet of vehicles this year. The city of Clearwater’s fleet was recognized as one of the “100 Best Fleets” in America for the fourth year in a row, by the National Fleet Management Association. In addition, the city approved its Green Fleet policy. Under this policy, the city has committed to transition its light-duty vehicles to those powered by alternative fuels, such as electric or hybrid vehicles. The city seeks to convert 100% of its light-duty fleet to these alternative fuels by 2040. These sustainability efforts represent our strategic directions of “Efficiency” and “Financial Responsibility.”

Flood Insurance Discounts

Thanks to our commitment to resiliency, residents in the city of Clearwater get a discount on flood insurance. The Engineering Department successfully completed Community Rating System recertification, which resulted in the city maintaining its classification. The result is continued savings to Clearwater homeowners of 20% on their flood insurance premiums. These efforts showcase our strategic directions of “Financial Responsibility,” “Develop and Promote our Brand,” and “Quality.”

Imagine Clearwater

The city finished design of and started construction on Imagine Clearwater, the downtown waterfront redevelopment project. The project features  an expansive park and recreational spaces, a gateway plaza and bluff walk that connects the park to downtown. Other amenities include a bay-walk promenade overlooking the Intracoastal Waterway, a lake area with picnic shelters, and an ocean-themed play area with an interactive pop-jet water feature, in addition to an amphitheater. The project represents our strategic directions of “Increase Economic Opportunity,” “Quality,” and “Develop and Promote our Brand.”

Crest Lake Park

The city transformed Crest Lake Park into Clearwater’s newest signature park, combining the physical beauty of a nature park in an urban setting. The park features multiple amenities for the community and visitors to enjoy. These include wider sidewalks around the lake and through the park, new lighting, irrigation, landscaping, four new play areas including a spray pad, restrooms, an arboretum, picnic shelters, two volleyball courts and improved parking.

We were thrilled to celebrate its grand opening this past Summer. Beautiful new parks like Crest Lake Park embody our strategic direction of “Quality.”

Cleveland Street Phase III

The Community Redevelopment Agency partnered with the Engineering Department to design and launch the Cleveland streetscape project. Part of this effort involves hosting public meetings to talk about the project with downtown neighbors, and other community outreach efforts. This is the final step in the investment into Cleveland Street, from the waterfront all the way to the Downtown Gateway, and coupled with Imagine Clearwater, it will give a warm welcome to those coming toward downtown Clearwater and Clearwater Beach.

This project reflects our strategic direction of “Increase Economic Opportunity,” “Foster Community Engagement,” and “Quality.”

Downtown Housing

The city is bringing more housing to its downtown. We have entered into three development agreements for apartments in our Community Redevelopment Area at 306 South Washington Street, 1250 Cleveland Street, 115 South Martin Luther King Junior Boulevard. We look forward to the groundbreaking for these exciting downtown housing developments. This accomplishment represents our strategic direction of “Diversify the Economic Base.”

North Greenwood CRA

A cross departmental team led by Economic Development worked with Clearwater Urban Leadership Coalition to determine the feasibility of creating a Community Redevelopment Area in the North Greenwood community. The results were reviewed by City Council and the Pinellas County Board of County Commissioners, and both groups supported the creation of a CRA plan for the area. This effort ties into our strategic directions of “Increase Economic Opportunity” and “Foster Community Engagement.”

Lost Graves Research Support

Librarians from the Clearwater Public Library System provided research assistance to the Clearwater Heights Reunion Committee, regarding lost African American gravesites in Clearwater. This information was invaluable to the Engineering department as they led an investigation of two such sites and confirmed the existence of graves on both sites. This action ties into our strategic direction of “Foster Community Engagement.”

Memvelope Program

The Clearwater Public Library System preserved nearly 4,000 treasured photos, slides, negatives, videos, films and audio recordings for patrons through its free Memvelope, or “memory envelope,” drop-off digitization service. This important program was awarded the Technology Innovation Award in the Citizens Category from Government Technology Special Districts Awards Program. This program showcase our strategic direction of “Foster Community Engagement.”

Art In The City

The Community Redevelopment Agency continued to focus on the arts, funding three new murals painted downtown, two murals painted on Cleveland Street traffic barriers, and a Wearable Art exhibit in vacant storefronts along Cleveland Street. In addition, the Planning & Development Department coordinated the painting of two intersections in conjunction with the Lake Belleview and Skycrest neighborhoods. The advancement of art throughout the city this year, promotes our strategic directions of “Foster Community Engagement.” and “Develop and Promote our Brand.”

Sports Tourism

The Parks & Recreation Department recruited and hosted 12 new sports tourism events in 2020/21, including the USA Artistic Swimming 2021 youth and junior international open, the U.S. and Mexican Softball Teams and the Dominican Republic Baseball Team for pre-Olympic trainings. The city also hosted the One Nation Slow-pitch Softball tournament, Glory Sports three-versus-three soccer tournament, and the Gasparilla Softball Classic. Parks & Recreation Department also hosted some Prep-Baseball Report and Prime-Time event showcases.

Our efforts to promote sports tourism embody our strategic directions of “Develop and Promote our Brand” and “Diversify the Economic Base.”

Mental Health Police Unit

Clearwater Police Department implemented a mental health unit within the agency, with two patrol officers working hand in hand with mental health professionals on calls where such services are needed. This allows those who need such services to be better served and it also allows other patrol units to be freed up for other calls for service. In addition to the two personnel who are dedicated to the unit, other officers have been trained as backups for such calls. This new program ties into our strategic directions of “Quality,” “Efficiency,” and Foster “Community Engagement.”

Police Body Cameras

Clearwater Police Department went live with its body camera program, equipping sergeants and officers in the Patrol Division with the cameras, in addition to specialty units such as SWAT and the Special Enforcement Unit. The cameras have worked to fulfill our continuing pledge of transparency to the community, and the new technology has been a benefit to citizens and officers alike. In some cases, we have been able to resolve citizen complaints by simply viewing the video from the body camera. This success promotes our strategic direction of “Safety” and “Efficiency.”

Trash & Recycling Vaults

The Solid Waste & Recycling Department installed three underground refuse system units at the city’s parking lot No. 36 on Clearwater Beach. More are planned throughout Clearwater. This innovative approach is an example of how departments are rethinking how they operate, and it reflects the strategic directions of “Efficiency,” “Quality,” and “Develop and Promote Our Brand.”

Red Tide Response 2021

The Parks & Recreation Department provided daily coordination with Clearwater Solid Waste and multiple other departments to address and minimize the red tide issue on Clearwater and Sand Key beaches. This hard work represents our strategic direction of “Quality.”

Staffing Successes

The city strengthened its staffing at a difficult time, when employers across the country are having a hard time hiring quality employees. The city coordinated the hiring of our next city manager and city attorney through multiple public processes. And the Human Resources Department recruited, hired and on-boarded more than 285 employees this year, and we promoted or conducted transfers of more than 375 staff.

In addition, the department developed an implementation plan for $15 per hour minimum wage requirement for employees. These actions reflect the strategic direction of “Quality.”

Clearwater 2045

This year, the Planning & Development Department conducted public outreach for Clearwater 2045, an update to our Comprehensive Plan. This plan details the long-term vision and future direction of our community, and it sets priorities and strategies for balanced growth through economic, social, physical, environmental and fiscal development.

This initiative will make sure that Clearwater continues to be a thriving community to work, live and play. It showcases our strategic direction of “Diversify the Economic Base.”

Audit Department
  • Performed various audits and reviews of city departments to determine compliance with laws, regulations, policies, procedures and best practices.
  • Provided advisory services to departments and personnel to help ensure processes were properly designed, program objectives were met, and risks were properly managed.
  • Provided recommendations at the conclusion of audits and advisory services and gave guidance that improved compliance, performance and risk management processes.
  • Implemented the risk-based plan developed in FY 2020 to determine the priorities of internal audit based on an understanding of the organization’s strategies, key business objectives, potential risks and risk management processes.
  • Incorporated the risk-based audit approach into current audits to better communicate potential risks to city leadership and departments.
  • Adopted the Association of Inspectors General, Principles and Standards for Offices of Inspector General, Quality Standards for Investigations to better address ethical concerns.
  • Updated the City Audit Department Policies and Procedures Manual. The new procedures are aligned with the International Professional Practices Framework (IPPF) established by the Institute of Internal Auditors.
  • Updated the methodology, processes and practices used to plan and conduct audits to better align the audit activities with the key strategic, operational, compliance and regulatory risks.
  • Evaluated the use of audit management software to ensure consistency in the audit function, increase efficiency of the audit process, and to improve the evaluation of risk across the organization.
Community Redevelopment Agency
  • Entered into three development agreements for apartments in the CRA at 306 S. Washington St., 1250 Cleveland St. and 115 S. Martin Luther King Jr. Ave.
  • Presented a new Opportunity Zone Prospectus, a 26-page comprehensive guide to investing in the city’s federal Opportunity Zone.
  • Partnered with Engineering to launch the Cleveland streetscape project, including facilitating public meetings and other community outreach efforts.
  • Launched a business assistance grant program in order to provide grant funding in an effort to combat downtown vacancies.
  • Continued to focus on the arts, funding three new murals painted downtown, two murals painted on Cleveland Street traffic barriers, and a Wearable Art exhibit in vacant storefronts along Cleveland Street.
  • Partnered with the library and sustainability departments to create StoryWalk® adventure downtown, a walking journey for children along Cleveland Street that provided an interactive way for children to have fun while learning to read.
Economic Development & Housing
  • Used federal grant funds to provide $26,170 in salary and administrative support for three public service agencies to support efforts to prevent and address root causes of homelessness. Additional general fund revenues were provided to six agencies to further support efforts to reduce street-level homelessness. Despite homeless shelters operating at a reduced capacity to comply with social distancing guidelines, or not accepting new clients to reduce the risk of exposure, Clearwater Police Department’s Downtown Bike Team transported 176 homeless individuals to Pinellas Safe Harbor, Pinellas Hope and the Homeless Empowerment Program. In addition, with assistance of the street outreach case manager from Directions for Living, 17 homeless people were placed in local shelters, such as Turning Point, ACTS Detox, Pinellas Hope and the Homeless Empowerment Program.
  • Used $128,826 in federal grant funds to provide salary and administrative support for 11 public service agencies delivering a variety of support services to low- and moderate-income families, veterans, seniors and others. Additional grant fund allocations included $51,575 for micro-enterprise and economic development activities, $145,048 for five public facility rehabilitation projects, and $5,530 to partner agencies to provide homebuyer education.
  • Preserved and provided a variety of quality affordable housing opportunities by using federal and state grant funds to process four down-payment assistance loans totaling $160,000. Funded the rehabilitation of seven units of a 240-unit affordable housing rehabilitation project, which will be completed next fiscal year. The department completed rehabilitation of a duplex in the North Greenwood community, which is now occupied by two families. Through partnership with Habitat for Humanity of Pinellas and West Pasco Counties, federal and state grant funds totaling $230,000 were used for acquisition and construction of two single-family homes. In addition, 79 individuals completed homebuyer education through partnerships with Tampa Bay Community Development Corporation, Bright Community Trust and Clearwater Neighborhood Housing Services.
  • Led the city’s pre-planning and communication efforts with Clearwater Urban Leadership Coalition and a cross-departmental team to determine the feasibility of creating a Community Redevelopment Area in the North Greenwood community. City Council supported the effort, and the Pinellas County Board of County Commissioners approved it and moved forward with the formation of the tax-increment financed district.
  • Entered into a funding agreement with Directions for Living to assist tenants in Prospect Towers, an affordable senior living community, facing displacement due to new ownership’s plan to increase rental rates.
  • Established a program to provide grants to eligible applicants for assessment and remediation of brownfields. The nearly $800,000 program is funded by the repayment of loans from a previous brownfield grant from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). Additionally, the department secured a $300,000 grant from the EPA to conduct brownfield assessments and remediation of contaminated sites.
  • Developed and implemented a marketing strategy for corporate relocation and expansion to Clearwater. Marketing activities included digital and print advertisements, development and distribution of an Opportunity Zone prospectus, sponsored media content placements in the Tampa Bay region and selected cities across the nation, enhanced real estate search features and data on the city’s website, and an email marketing campaign targeting site selectors and commercial brokerage firms. Analysis of marketing data shows the email campaign has yielded a 25.9 percent open rate, and traffic counts to the city’s “Invest Clearwater” page shows 82 percent bounce rate of unique visitors to externally hosted Clearwater content. Both figures indicate a growing awareness of economic development opportunities.
  • Delivered resources to entrepreneurs and small businesses through Clearwater Business SPARK, a collaboration of partners. As a founding partner, the city’s Economic Development Division expanded the SPARK network to 11 organizations and provided approximately $53,000 in direct and indirect marketing initiatives, including earned and paid media, social media, newsletters, website management, digital and print advertising, promotional items and other related activities. From these efforts, staff engaged more than 200 local small businesses and entrepreneurs and provided direct assistance to 17. The department provided funding support to Prospera and Tampa Bay Black Business Investment Corp. to assist 26 entrepreneurs and small businesses in targeted areas, including North Greenwood, Lake Belleview and Drew Street corridor.
  • Provided direct assistance to 34 businesses as part of the city’s Business Retention & Expansion program. Types of assistance included site selection, permitting and referrals for workforce development and recruitment, manufacturing support services and foreign trade assistance.
  • Continued to contract with Bandwagon, LLC, a tourism-focused public relations and marketing firm, to enhance the city’s profile as a premier destination for tourism and small group business travel. Despite the pandemic, more than $3.9 million in earned media was secured from press trips and through proactive media relations efforts in publications such as TravelingMom.com, MSN, Travel Awaits, The Epoch Times, The Christian Science Monitor, The Points Guy, and more. Bandwagon hosted in-market Cory Lee of Curb Free with Cory Lee, a global, award-winning travel influencer. Videos and other social media content posted during the trip generated 3,542 total likes, 120 shares, and 344 comments. His coverage continues to be syndicated to other outlets beyond his blog and exceeds more than $2 million in value. Usage of the tourism website (visitclearwaterflorida.com) was up 83 percent compared to the previous fiscal year.

Fire & Rescue
  • Obtained the prestigious Insurance Services Office Rating 1, which is the best rating fire departments can get. The scale goes from 1 to 10, with one being the best possible rating and 10 the worst. The lower the rating, the lower the insurance premiums should be for Clearwater residents. A Rating 1 is a sign of superior fire protection services.
  • Welcomed beach lifeguards to the Clearwater Fire & Rescue team. Beach lifeguards provide basic life support services, such as automatic external defibrillator provisions, oxygen administration, hemorrhage control and patient assessments. They work with an assortment of Pinellas County lifesaving equipment to medically address the vast array of emergency medical situations surrounding the Gulf/beach environment, which include lightning strikes, water rescues, water-related bites and stings, and other prevalent medical emergencies.

The AEDs are portable, life-saving devices designed to treat people experiencing sudden cardiac arrest, a medical condition where the heart stops beating suddenly and unexpectedly. They are required equipment on the fire department’s rescue units and advanced life support fire engines. The fire department oversees the LIFEPAK AEDs currently in key city facilities such as City Hall offices, Municipal Services Building, libraries, police and fire department facilities.

Police Department
  • Continued to incorporate the Real Time Crime Center (RTCC) as a part of daily operations with the Communications Section and the Patrol Division to enhance officer safety and improve criminal case closures. The RTCC has greatly expanded its operational capacity in the last year, including installing Pan Tilt Zoom IP Cameras in District 1 (Clearwater Beach), adding two grant-funded mobile surveillance platforms, and implementing License Plate Reader technology. These increased capabilities have provided the Patrol Division with direct and actionable intelligence on more than 1,000 calls for service, including the arrest of more than 100 suspects. Proactive investigative work by members of the RTCC has led to the arrest of 28 additional suspects, and the recovery of more than $250,000 in stolen goods.
  • Went live with its body camera program, equipping sergeants and officers in the Patrol Division with the cameras, in addition to specialty units such as SWAT and the Special Enforcement Unit. The cameras have worked to fulfill our continuing pledge of transparency to the community, and the new technology has been a benefit to citizens and officers alike. In some cases, we have been able to resolve citizen complaints by simply viewing the video from the body camera.
  • All sworn personnel completed the Active Bystander for Law Enforcement training. The goal is to prepare officers to reinforce the police culture in which active peer intervention prevents misconduct, avoids mistakes and promotes health and wellness.
  • Implemented a Mental Health Unit within the agency, with two patrol officers working hand in hand with mental health professionals on calls where such services are needed. This allows those who need such services to be better served and it also allows other patrol units to be freed up for other calls for service. In addition to the two personnel who are dedicated to the unit, other officers have been trained as backups for such calls.
