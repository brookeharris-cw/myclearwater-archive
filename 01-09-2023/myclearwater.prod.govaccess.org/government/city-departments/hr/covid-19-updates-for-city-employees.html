On this page, you will find resources and communications intended for city of Clearwater employees. Please share relevant resources with employees who do not have computer access.

July 5, 2022

Effective immediately, city employees are no longer required to quarantine if they have been exposed to someone who has tested positive for COVID-19. However, if exposed, they must wear a mask for 10 days from the last exposure date and continue to self-monitor for COVID like symptoms. If symptoms develop, remain home, report to your supervisor and it is recommended that you get tested at that time.

If you test positive for COVID-19 regardless of your vaccination status, you must stay home for 5 days. You may return to work on day 6 if your symptoms have subsided and you are fever free. Please refer to the COVID Quarantine and Isolation Calculator to determine the isolation time frame. https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/your-health/quarantine-isolation.html#.  Healthcare providers do not fall under the General Public category and will still be required to follow the 7- and 10-day isolation guidance for COVID-19 positives.

If you do not test positive for COVID, stay home until you are symptom free. If you are feeling ill in any way, have symptoms of COVID and / or not feeling well, please do not report to work, contact your supervisor, and follow the city sick leave policy. You can check for COVID symptoms on the self-checker at the following CDC link: https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/symptoms-testing/coronavirus-self-checker.html.

Those who are currently out on quarantine due to a positive COVID exposure may return to work if they are still symptom free. They must, however, continue to monitor their symptoms for the remaining 10 days from their last exposure and they must also continue wearing a mask for the same period of time.

Please contact your supervisor if you need further clarification on this updated protocol. Thank you and stay safe.

Jevon 
Jevon A. Graham, CFO, MIFireE 
Division Chief of Emergency Management 

COVID-19 Exposure Decisions: Being Sick & Returning To Work

Updated Dec. 30, 2021

What if I am showing symptoms of FLU or COVID-19, should I come to work? 

  • No, If an employee is showing symptoms of flu or COVID-19, that employee should notify their supervisor by phone and stay home and not work. 

What if someone who lives with me is showing signs of FLU or COVID-19, do I need to tell my supervisor? 

  • Yes, If someone that an employee is cohabitating with is showing signs of FLU or COVID-19, that employee should inform their supervisor, and stay home if not fully vaccinated. Fully vaccinated is defined as 2 weeks after their second dose in a 2-dose series, such as the Pfizer or Moderna vaccines, or 2 weeks after a single-dose vaccine, such as Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen vaccine. If you are within 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or within 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J or have received a booster you are not required to quarantine.  Proof of vaccination is required. 

What if I have recently been tested for COVID-19, do I need to notify my supervisor? 

  • Yes, If an employee is being tested for virus that causes COVID-19, that employee is obligated to let their supervisor know. Depending on the circumstances and subsequent follow-up, that employee may need to self-quarantine if not fully vaccinated. Fully vaccinated is defined as 2 weeks after their second dose in a 2-dose series, such as the Pfizer or Moderna vaccines, or 2 weeks after a single-dose vaccine, such as Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen vaccine. If you are within 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or within 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J or have received a booster you are not required to quarantine. Proof of vaccination is required. 

What if someone that I live with is being tested for COVID-19, do I need to notify my supervisor? 

  • Yes, If someone that an employee is cohabitating with is being tested for the virus that causes COVID-19, that employee is obligated to let their supervisor know. Depending on the circumstances and subsequent follow-up, that employee may need to self-quarantine if not fully vaccinated. Fully vaccinated is defined as 2 weeks after their second dose in a 2-dose series, such as the Pfizer or Moderna vaccines, or 2 weeks after a single-dose vaccine, such as Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen vaccine. If you are within 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or within 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J or have received a booster you are not required to quarantine. Proof of vaccination is required. 

What if I test positive for COVID-19? 

  • As per the most recently published CDC guidelines, if test results show a positive result, then that employee shall isolate themselves and not return to work for at least 5 days since the employee first began to feel symptoms or, if asymptomatic, since the day the test was administered. In both cases, the employee must be symptom free without the use of medication unless cleared by a licensed medical provider. 

If I'm symptomatic and my COVID-19 test is negative, what should I do? 

  • As per the most recently published CDC guidelines, if test results show a negative result, then that employee must be symptom free without the use of medication unless cleared by a licensed medical provider before that employee can return to work. 

I was directly exposed to a person who tested positive for COVID-19. What should I do? 

  • If an employee feels they have been exposed to a person who recently tested positive for the virus that causes COVID-19, then that employee must notify their immediate supervisor. Depending on the circumstances and subsequent follow-up, that employee may need to self-quarantine if not fully vaccinated. Fully vaccinated is defined as 2 weeks after their second dose in a 2-dose series, such as the Pfizer or Moderna vaccines, or 2 weeks after a single-dose vaccine, such as Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen vaccine. If you are within 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or within 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J or have received a booster you are not required to quarantine.  Proof of vaccination is required. 

What if I am fully vaccinated and exposed to a COVID-19 positive person? 

  • If an employee is fully vaccinated and is directly exposed to a confirmed COVID-19 positive individual and not symptomatic, the employee does NOT need to quarantine.  However, it is strongly recommended that fully vaccinated people get tested 5-7 days after their exposure, even if they don’t have symptoms, and wear a mask indoors in public for 10 days following exposure or until their test result is negative. Proof of vaccination is required.  

How long do I need to quarantine if I am not vaccinated or partially vaccinated? 

  • The general population CDC guidance provides that you should stay home for 14 5 days after your last contact with a person who has COVID-19. After that, continue to wear a mask around others for 5 additional days. Watch for fever (100.4◦F), cough, shortness of breath, or other symptoms of COVID-19. If possible, stay away from people you live with, especially people who are at higher risk of getting very sick from COVID-19. 

What does fully vaccinated mean?

  • Fully vaccinated is defined as 2 weeks after their second dose in a 2-dose series, such as the Pfizer or Moderna vaccines, or 2 weeks after a single-dose vaccine, such as Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen vaccine.
  • If you are within 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or within 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J or have received a booster you are not required to quarantine.
  • If you are passed 6 months of receiving a 2nd dose of Pfizer or Moderna, or passed 2 Months of receiving a single dose of J & J and have not received a booster you are required to quarantine. 

Who is considered a close contact/direct exposure of someone with COVID-19?

  • For COVID-19, a close contact is anyone (masked or unmasked) who was within 6 feet of an infected person (masked or unmasked) for a total of 15 minutes or more over a 24-hour period (for example, three individual 5-minute exposures for a total of 15 minutes).  
  • An infected person can spread COVID-19 starting from 2 days before they have any symptoms (or if they are asymptomatic, 2 days before their specimen that tested positive was collected) until they meet the criteria for discontinuing home isolation. Again, it is still considered close contact when one or both persons is wearing a mask.  

When should I isolate?

  • You isolate when you have been infected with the virus, even if you don’t have symptoms. Isolation is used to separate people infected with COVID-19 from those who are not infected. 
  • People who are in isolation should stay home until it’s safe for them to be around others. At home, anyone sick or infected should separate from others, stay in a specific “sick room” or area, and use a separate bathroom (if available). 

Questions?

  • If you have any questions, reach out to Chief Jevon Graham (727) 562-4334, ext. 3205.
Symptoms of COVID-19

Updated Dec. 30, 2021

COVID-19 affects different people in different ways. Most infected people will develop mild to moderate illness and recover without hospitalization. The CDC does not list different symptoms for the Delta variant or others.

Take a self-assessment with CDC

Most common symptoms include:

  • Fever
  • Dry cough
  • Tiredness

Less common symptoms:

  • Aches and pains
  • Sore throat
  • Diarrhea
  • Conjunctivitis
  • Headache
  • Loss of taste or smell
  • a rash on skin, or discoloration of fingers or toes

Serious symptoms:

  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Chest pain or pressure
  • Loss of speech of movement

Seek immediate medical attention if you have serious symptoms. Always call before visiting your doctor or health facility.

On average it takes five to six days from when someone is infected with the virus for symptoms to show. However, it can take up to 14 days. 

CDC: Symptoms https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/symptoms-testing/symptoms.html

Face Coverings: Not Required

As of Oct. 20, 2021, Micah Maxwell informed staff that the city lifted the requirement for employees to wear masks while at work. Employees are still asked to maintain social distancing and to stay more than 6 feet apart as much as possible.

Employees who would feel more comfortable continuing to wear masks may do so, and all employees are expected to be respectful of one another’s individual choice.

Understand that our situation remains very fluid, and should either community or city numbers begin to rise, I will revert us back to mask-wearing, so it will be incumbent on all of us to use good judgment in our work and personal lives and to do our best to maintain social distancing protocols.

Travel Restrictions & Guidelines

Domestic Travel

  • Current & Constantly Updated (Daily) Florida Traveler Information is here at www.visitflorida.com/more/current-travel-safety-information/covid-travel-safety-information/

State and Local Travel Restrictions or Orders

Like Florida, some state and local governments may have in place travel restrictions, stay at home or shelter-in-place orders, mandated quarantines upon arrival, or even state border checkpoints while you are traveling. For more information and travel guidance, check with the state or local health department where you are, along your route, and at your planned destination. Just because there are no restrictions at the time you plan to leave does not mean there won’t be restrictions in place when you arrive. 

If you are thinking about traveling away from your local community, ask:

  • Is COVID-19 spreading where you’re going? You can get infected while traveling.
  • Is COVID-19 spreading in your community? Even if you don’t have symptoms, you can spread COVID-19 to others while traveling.
  • Will you, or those you are traveling with, be within 6 feet of others during or after your trip? Being within 6 feet of others increases your chances of getting infected and infecting others.
  • Are you or those you are traveling with more likely to get very ill from COVID-19? Older adults and people of any age who have a serious underlying medical condition are at higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19.
  • Do you live with someone who is more likely to get very ill from COVID-19? If you get infected while traveling you can spread COVID-19 to loved ones when you return, even if you don’t have symptoms.
  • Does the state or local government where you live or at your destination require you to stay home for 14 days after traveling? Some state and local governments may require people who have recently traveled to stay home for 14 days.
